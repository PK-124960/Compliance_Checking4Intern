Problem Statement & Motivation

🏛️ The Real-World Gap

  • Universities (e.g., AIT) maintain extensive P&P documents in natural language
  • Policy administrators manually interpret; no systematic validation exists
  • Prior ML approaches achieve only 75–85% accuracy and require heavy feature engineering
  • No end-to-end pipeline from NL policy → executable validation constraint exists

❓ Three Interconnected Challenges

  • Rule Identification: Distinguish normative (deontic) from descriptive text
  • Logic Sufficiency: Is FOL+deontic adequate, or is SOL/HOL required?
  • Translation Feasibility: Can formalized logic → executable SHACL constraints?
Core thesis claim: LLMs can identify policy rules from P&P documents; FOL with deontic operators (O, P, F) is sufficient for institutional policy formalization; and systematic FOL-to-SHACL translation produces executable validation shapes — forming a complete, corpus-agnostic end-to-end pipeline without higher-order logics.

🤖 Models Tested

🔬 Validation

pySHACL + GraphDB · F1 = 0.866 against gold-standard shapes · 10-run stability

Discussion — Why & What It Means

⚡ Why Mistral 7B Beat Llama 3.1 70B

  • Architectural efficiency: Grouped-Query Attention (GQA) focuses compute on classification-relevant features
  • Instruction tuning quality: Mistral's tuning is optimized for structured JSON + classification — precisely our task
  • Specialization vs. generalization: Large general models may "overthink" simple binary tasks
  • Practical implication: Use 7–30B instruction-tuned models for specialized NLP; don't assume bigger = better

📐 Why FOL Is Sufficient

  • Institutional policies express concrete obligations about individuals, not abstract classes of predicates
  • No tested rule required quantification over predicates (SOL) or λ-abstractions (HOL)
  • Resolves a theoretical debate: "Do regulatory domains need HOL?" — empirical answer: No, for institutional policies
  • Keeps reasoning tractable and interpretable

🔧 Engineering > Theory

  • 8.3pp improvement (v1→v2) came from better JSON parsing + longer response, not better logic
  • M4 jump from F1=0.000 to F1=0.866 came from vocabulary injection, not smarter LLM reasoning
  • Largest single improvement: fixing evaluation infrastructure (gold standard re-annotation + code bugs)
Key insight: Structured output enforcement, ontology guidance, and robust parsing matter more than model expressiveness.

📊 Cross-Domain & Reliability Evidence

  • Multi-LLM agreement (5 orgs): Fleiss' κ = 0.635 (Mistral, Llama 3.1:8B, Qwen3:8B, Gemma3:4B, Phi4-mini)
  • Intra-annotator: Cohen's κ = 0.891 (almost perfect) — mitigates single-annotator limitation
  • LexDeMod (N=200 lease clauses): Obligation F1=0.569 — transfers across legal domains; Permission F1=0.038 — vocabulary mismatch causes degradation
  • Output stability: 10 runs, hash-identical under fixed seed + LLM cache

🔑 Limitations to Acknowledge

Single annotator: Mitigated by multi-LLM study (κ=0.635) and intra-annotator reliability (κ=0.891)
English/AIT only: Cross-domain test with GDPR (D5) and LexDeMod (D4) provides partial evidence of generalizability
Permission challenge: "may" ambiguity (descriptive vs. normative) remains at 50% type accuracy; prompt engineering raised it to 70%

Committee Q&A Preparation — Core Questions

Why did Mistral 7B outperform Llama 3.1 70B? Is this a general finding or specific to your task?
Methodology

This is task-specific, not a general finding. Three factors explain it:

  • Architectural efficiency (GQA): Mistral's grouped-query attention reduces memory overhead while maintaining representational quality — ideal for structured binary classification
  • Instruction-tuning alignment: Mistral's tuning closely matches our task (JSON output + classification reasoning). Hoffmann et al.'s scaling laws show that training data quality matters more than raw parameters for specific tasks
  • Overgeneralization: Larger models trained for diverse reasoning may "overthink" simple binary deontic detection

The practical implication: for specialized institutional NLP tasks, 7–30B instruction-tuned models are preferable over 70B+ models, offering better cost, latency, and performance.

Your H1 hypothesis required ≥95% accuracy but you achieved 93.81%. How do you handle this?
Critical

This is acknowledged and addressed transparently. The distinction is important:

  • The 99% identification rate is a coverage metric — how many of the 97 sentences the model processed and flagged as policy rules (1 missed)
  • The 93.81% validated accuracy is the agreement metric against the gold standard (Cohen's κ = 0.7636)
  • The H1 threshold of 95% was set ex-ante based on prior ML literature (75–85% baseline). Our result of 93.81% represents an 8.81–18.81pp improvement over prior art and exceeds the rule-based regex baseline (82.5%)

The gap (1.19pp) is driven by "should" ambiguity (6 errors) — a semantic annotation challenge, not a model failure. We updated the conclusion to reflect this nuance accurately.

You're a single annotator. How can we trust the gold standard?
Critical

Three converging lines of evidence validate the gold standard:

  • Conservative inclusion criteria: Only sentences with unambiguous deontic markers (must, shall, may, cannot) and clear subject-action structure were included; borderline cases excluded
  • Multi-LLM agreement study: 5 independent LLMs from 5 organizations (Mistral AI, Meta, Alibaba, Google, Microsoft) classify 50 sampled rules → Fleiss' κ = 0.635 (substantial agreement with the human annotation)
  • Intra-annotator reliability: Re-annotated same document 30 days later → Cohen's κ = 0.891 (almost perfect) — demonstrates annotation consistency over time

Together, these provide triangulated evidence that the gold standard reflects a valid, consistent annotation of deontic content in AIT policies.

Is First-Order Logic really sufficient? What about complex conditional rules?
Theory

Yes, for institutional policies — and here's why:

  • FOL expresses universally quantified conditionals: ∀x. Condition(x) → Obligated(x, action)
  • Complex compound conditions are handled by conjunction: Student(x) ∧ FullTime(x) ∧ ¬ExtensionGranted(x)
  • We systematically tested 5 higher-order construct patterns (predicate quantification, set comprehension, function variables, λ-expressions, recursive definitions) across all 81 rules — zero instances found

Institutional policies express specific obligations about specific agents, not meta-level rules about rules. Description Logic lacks explicit deontic operators; FOL+deontic is the natural fit.

Committee Q&A — Deep & Challenging Questions

The SHACL shapes F1 dropped from 0.000 to 0.866 — but that means 13.4% of shapes are still wrong. What are these errors and are they acceptable?
Results

The 13.4% "error" breaks down into two distinct categories:

  • 8 inverted cases (false violations): Upstream deontic type misclassification cascades — e.g., obligation classified as prohibition, generating sh:maxCount 0 instead of sh:minCount 1. Root cause: LLM type confusion, not structural SHACL error
  • 12 too-permissive cases: Correct deontic type but insufficient constraint specificity (e.g., missing a numerical bound or secondary condition)

Precision = 0.977 means that when the pipeline generates a constraint, it is almost always structurally correct. The recall gap (0.778) reflects missing coverage. For a research prototype, F1=0.866 is acceptable; production deployment would require iterative Corpus Adapter refinement to close this gap.

Output stability (M5 = 100%) is claimed, but isn't this trivial with a fixed seed and cached LLM?
Validity

You raise a fair point, and the thesis is explicit about this:

  • M5 measures hash-equality of generated SHACL across 10 runs under controlled conditions (seed=42, temperature=0.1, populated cache, pinned model digest)
  • This satisfies the procedural replication prerequisite — a researcher can reproduce the exact output from the same input. This is meaningful for scientific accountability
  • M5 does NOT measure semantic correctness. That is argued separately via M4 (F1=0.866), inter-rater reliability (κ=0.7636), and the TDD suite (480 parametrized tests)
  • Without the cache populated (cold start), the pipeline would show variation — the thesis explicitly labels M5 as "output stability under controlled conditions," not an unconditional reproducibility guarantee
Why did you choose zero-shot prompting? Wouldn't few-shot examples improve performance significantly?
Design Choice
  • Empirical evidence from literature: Goknil et al. (2024, PAPEL) found that for privacy policy analysis, "simpler prompts often perform as well as complex ones across advanced language models"
  • Our preliminary experiments: Adding few-shot examples did not improve classification accuracy above the 99% zero-shot baseline while increasing prompt length and inference time
  • Stronger scientific test: Zero-shot probes the model's inherent deontic understanding, providing more generalizable evidence
  • Prompt sensitivity analysis: We tested 4 prompt variants on 100 disagreement cases (N=100 sample). The zero-shot baseline (V0) achieved the highest agreement (85%, κ=0.101); additional engineering marginally degraded performance (Δκ < 0.03)
Your permission classification accuracy is only 50%. Doesn't this undermine the practical utility of the pipeline?
Results
  • The 50% is for deontic type assignment (obligation vs. permission vs. prohibition) — not for the binary rule detection task (99% identification)
  • The challenge is a well-documented linguistic problem: "may" can express normative permission ("Students may reside off-campus") or factual possibility ("Deadlines may be extended") — Kratzer's modal semantics framework
  • Remediation: Explicit definition prompting raised permission accuracy from 0% to 70% (h=1.98, large effect, p=0.023) on the targeted subset
  • Practical impact: In SHACL terms, a misclassified permission generates sh:Info (informational) instead of sh:Violation — a softer failure, not a wrong constraint. Obligatory constraints (the highest-stakes) achieve 89.1% type accuracy
How does your pipeline compare to existing commercial tools like IBM OpenPages or similar GRC platforms?
Contribution
  • Commercial GRC platforms (IBM OpenPages, MetricStream) perform manual rule mapping — they do not automatically extract and formalize rules from NL documents
  • Research predecessors (LexGLUE, LegalBench, PAPEL) address isolated components: either classification only or annotation only — none produce executable SHACL constraints
  • Our unique position: Complete NL → FOL → SHACL pipeline on institutional (not just legal) policies, with explicit deontic semantics preservation and ontology-guided generation
  • The Corpus Adapter enables deployment across institutions without retraining — a portability advantage over domain-specific trained models
The Student Handbook has 45 pages but yields only 10 policy rules — much lower density than other documents. Why?
Data
  • The Student Handbook includes significant non-normative content: appendices, FAQs, procedural guidance, tables, and institutional descriptions that do not contain deontic statements
  • Only 10 sentences in the Handbook contained unambiguous modal verbs (must, shall, may) with clear subject-action normative structure under our conservative inclusion criteria
  • Other documents (e.g., FB-6-1-2 Accommodation: 12 pages, 29 rules) are regulation-dense by design — every sentence specifies a rule or procedure
  • This reflects a genuine difference in document genre, not annotation error. A future improvement: use document-type-aware segmentation to handle mixed-genre documents more effectively
What is the main limitation that could prevent real-world deployment of this system today?
Future Work

Three deployment blockers, in priority order:

  • Manual Corpus Adapter curation (Stage 0 problem): Currently, an institutional ontology must be hand-curated before the Corpus Adapter can inject domain vocabulary. This is the primary bottleneck for rapid deployment to new institutions. Future work: automated ontology bootstrapping from existing institutional databases
  • Permission classification accuracy (50% type accuracy): While binary detection is 99%, the 50% permission type accuracy could generate incorrect SHACL constraint types (sh:Info when sh:Violation was intended, or vice versa)
  • Static analysis only: The pipeline performs offline validation, not real-time enforcement. Integration with live student information systems (e.g., PostgreSQL-backed) is a runtime engineering challenge beyond this thesis scope
